Getting Started

  1. Complete Your Rider Profile Tell us about your riding background, goals, current level, and what you're working on. This gives the AI essential context about who you are as a rider.
  2. Complete Your Horse Profile(s) Enter your horse's details‚Äîbreed, age, temperament, training level, strengths, and any quirks. If you ride multiple horses, create a profile for each.
  3. Bookmark the Post-Ride Debrief on Your Phone This is your primary daily tool. Save it to your phone's home screen for instant access at the barn. You'll use this after every ride‚Äîthe memories are freshest right after you dismount.
  4. Bookmark the Reflection Form on Your Phone This is for deeper reflections. Save it alongside the debrief form. We suggest weekly as a starting rhythm, but reflect as often as you like‚Äîthere's no limit.
  5. Try Voice Input All forms support voice input on mobile. Tap the microphone icon in any text field‚Äîthis is a game-changer for quick entries while untacking or capturing observations on the go.

üí° Pro Tip: Mobile-First Setup

Your Daily & Weekly Workflow

After Every Ride (2‚Äì5 Minutes)

Open the Post-Ride Debrief on your phone while you're still at the barn. Not every section needs an answer‚Äîfollow what's meaningful for today's ride.

Key debrief features: Set your ride intentions before you mount (what are you focusing on today?) and note whether you met them afterward. The debrief also captures your rider state and horse state‚Äîenergy, mood, focus, tension‚Äîgoing into and coming out of the ride. These states are powerful data points: over time, the AI can show you which conditions consistently produce your best work.
Breakthrough: What worked
Aha Moment: What clicked
Connection: Your horse
Validation: Feedback
Challenge: What's hard
Feel: Body awareness

Weekly (10‚Äì15 Minutes)

Choose one Reflection from the six categories. These dig deeper into specific moments, challenges, or realizations. The prompts help trigger memories and guide your thinking. If a prompt doesn't resonate, pass it‚Äîyou get three passes per session.

Weekly is a great rhythm, but it's not a limit. Reflect whenever inspiration strikes and as often as you like. Had a breakthrough lesson on Monday and a frustrating ride on Thursday? Capture both. The more reflections you record, the richer the patterns the AI can find.

The More You Collect, The More You Discover

Think of data collection like building a puzzle. Five pieces show you a corner. Twenty pieces reveal a pattern. Fifty pieces show you the full picture. Be generous with your reflections and debriefs‚Äîeach one adds another data point that helps the AI identify what's really going on beneath the surface.

The Six Reflection Categories

Each week, pick one category that resonates with where you are right now. Over time, aim to capture reflections across all six categories so the AI has a complete picture of your riding experience.

üí° How Prompts Work

Each category has 25 prompts to trigger specific memories. When you select a category, you'll see one prompt. If it doesn't resonate, you can pass it (3 passes per session) to see another. The goal is to capture meaningful moments, not force responses to prompts that don't fit your experience.

Setting Up Your Profiles

Your Rider Profile and Horse Profile(s) are the foundation of personalized analysis. They give the AI the context it needs to make your coaching insights specific to you rather than generic advice.

üèá Rider Profile

Your riding background, current level, goals, training frequency, instructor information, and what you're working on. This helps the AI calibrate its feedback to your experience and ambitions. Update it whenever your circumstances change‚Äînew instructor, new goals, different training focus.

üê¥ Horse Profile(s)

Your horse's breed, age, training level, temperament, strengths, areas of development, and any quirks or considerations. The more the AI knows about your horse, the better it can interpret patterns in your ride debriefs. Create a profile for each horse you ride regularly‚Äîthe AI tracks patterns for each partnership separately.

üí° Keep Profiles Current

Profiles aren't "set and forget." As your training evolves, your horse progresses, or your goals shift, update your profiles. Current profiles mean more relevant analysis.

Events: Logging & Preparation

Two forms work together to help you get the most from shows, clinics, lessons, and other significant moments in your dressage journey.

üìã Event Preparation Form

Use this before an upcoming show, clinic, or significant lesson that's within the next six months. (Beyond six months, plans change too much to be practical.) It helps you think through your goals, preparation plan, potential challenges, and what you want to focus on. Structured preparation leads to better performance and‚Äîjust as importantly‚Äîbetter learning from the experience afterward.

  • Shows & competitions: Define your goals beyond just scores‚Äîwhat do you want to demonstrate? What are you testing?
  • Clinics: Identify specific questions and focus areas so you arrive with clarity
  • Significant lessons: Prepare when you're working on something new or challenging with your instructor

üìÖ Journey Event Log

Use this after (or during) significant events to create a record. Log shows, clinics, vet visits, rider or horse health issues, environmental changes (new barn, footing changes, seasonal shifts), farrier visits, new equipment, or training milestones. The form captures not just what happened, but the impact and significance of each event on your journey. These entries become data points the AI uses to correlate events with changes in your riding patterns.

The power of pairing these forms: When you prepare for an event AND log what actually happened, the AI can analyze the gap between intention and outcome‚Äîone of the most revealing patterns in rider development.

üí° Don't Forget the "Small" Events

A farrier visit, a saddle fitting, a sore back, allergy season, even a change in turnout schedule can affect your riding. Log these too‚Äîthey often explain patterns that would otherwise seem mysterious.

Observation Form

Beyond your own riding, there's tremendous value in capturing insights from watching others. The Observation Form lets you document learning moments when you:

  • Attend a clinic as an auditor or participant
  • Watch your trainer ride your horse
  • Observe training sessions at your barn
  • Audit a show or competition
  • Watch instructional videos or competitions online

How It Works

The Observation Form is structured similarly to your reflections, allowing you to capture multiple learning moments from a single event using five categories (Feel is not included since it captures your own body awareness while riding):

Breakthrough: Technical or training breakthroughs you observed
Aha Moment: What resonated and how you could apply it
Connection: Horse-rider partnership dynamics
Validation: Concepts confirmed or validated
Obstacle: Challenges and how they were overcome

Why This Matters

Observation data enriches your overall pattern analysis. When the AI sees both what you experience in the saddle AND what resonates when you watch others, it can identify gaps between what you recognize intellectually and what you're implementing practically. This reveals blind spots and accelerates learning transfer.

üí° Best Use Cases

Most valuable: Watching your trainer ride your own horse (direct application to your partnership), attending clinics with instructors whose methods you want to understand better, or watching riders just one level above you (close enough to be immediately applicable).

Self-Assessments

Periodic self-assessments give you and the AI a structured snapshot of where you are right now‚Äîboth as a rider and physically.

üìä Rider Self-Assessment

A structured review of your riding skills, confidence levels, and areas of focus across different aspects of dressage. Taking this periodically lets you track how your self-perception evolves over time‚Äîand the AI can compare your self-assessment against patterns in your ride debriefs for deeper insight.

üèãÔ∏è Physical Self-Assessment

An honest look at your physical readiness for riding‚Äîflexibility, core strength, balance, fitness, and any limitations. Physical factors have a huge impact on riding performance, and tracking them helps the AI connect the dots between your body and your rides.

üí° When to Reassess

Take your first self-assessments when you start, then revisit them monthly or quarterly. They're especially valuable after a break from riding, recovering from an injury, changing training intensity, or starting work with a new horse.

Getting the Most Value

Write for Future You, Not for Perfection

Three specific sentences beat one vague paragraph every time. "Slow to pick up right lead" is infinitely more useful than "horse was stiff." The AI finds patterns in specifics, not generalities.

Use Voice Input at the Barn

On mobile, tap the microphone icon in any text field. Talk through your observations while untacking‚Äîit's faster than typing and captures details you'd forget by the time you get home.

Set Intentions, Then Track Results

Before your ride, note what you're planning to focus on. After, note what actually happened. The gap between intention and result reveals where you need support.

Be Honest About the Hard Stuff

The AI learns most from obstacles and challenges. If you only record wins, the analysis won't have enough signal to help you break through plateaus.

Use "Compared To" Language

Adding comparison takes 5 seconds but multiplies value: "More balanced than last Tuesday" or "Stiffer than warmup" or "Better after the walk break." Context creates patterns.

Pair Preparation with Follow-Up

Use the Event Preparation form before shows and clinics, then log the event and complete a debrief afterward. This before/after pairing is one of the richest sources of insight the AI can work with.

üí° The 2-Minute Rule

If completing a form feels like it's taking more than 5 minutes, you're overthinking it. Capture the key details and move on. Consistent 2-minute entries beat sporadic 20-minute essays.
