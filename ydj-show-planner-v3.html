Test Directives — What Judges Are Looking For
Regularity & Freedom — Pure, clear rhythm with engagement and elasticity in all gaits
Collection & Self-Carriage — The horse carries itself; the rider's hand supports but does not hold the balance
Impulsion — Stored energy that can be released into any movement; not speed
Straightness — The horse's hind feet track up on the forefeet; no haunches trailing or shoulders escaping
Submission — Willing, attentive, and harmonious; tension in the jaw or neck reads immediately as resistance
General Impression — The overall picture: harmony, ease, and the impression that horse and rider are working together effortlessly

Movement Coefficient Why It Matters
Extended trot ×2 A 6 becomes 12 points; a 7 becomes 14. The most visible movement in the test.
Extended canter ×2 Judges see this on the long diagonal. Uphill tendency and clear return to collection are both scored here.
Halt — immobility — rein back ×2 Often overlooked in prep. Square halt, 3 seconds immobility, 5 clean steps back, seamless forward transition.
Submission ×2 Collective mark. Jaw tension, resistance, and behind-the-leg moments all penalize this score across the whole test.
General Impression ×2 Collective mark. The judge's overall read of harmony, ease, and rider effectiveness. Hard to win back once lost.
Score Strategy — What This Means for Your Prep
📌 Extended trot is your biggest lever. It's doubled AND highly visible. A relaxed, forward, uphill extension from a horse that's truly through scores dramatically better than a tight, spectacular one.
📌 The halt/rein back is worth practicing as a unit. It's often treated as a transition moment, not a scored movement. 5 steps back on a straight line, no shuffling, is entirely trainable.
📌 Jaw tension hurts your submission score all test long. Rocket's tendency to tighten early means your warm-up protocol has a direct effect on your highest-value collective mark.
