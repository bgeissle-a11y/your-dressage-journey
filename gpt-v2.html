The First Five Minutes — Building a diagnostic lens
Read what your horse is offering today before your training agenda takes over.
🔍
The Opening Scan
Before any collection or lateral work — 5 min
Pre-ride
▾
In the first five minutes of every ride, before attempting any collection or lateral work, conduct a mental inventory: How tight is his jaw? How does the left rein feel vs. the right? Is he offering to stretch down or bracing up? Name one specific thing he's showing you today before you ask for anything demanding. Log it in your debrief "Horse Notices" field.

The Mid-Ride Checkpoint
Halfway through — pause on loose rein walk
During ride
▾
Halfway through your ride, pause for 10 seconds on a loose rein walk. Ask yourself: "Is what I planned still serving this horse today, or do I need to adjust?" If jaw tension persists (Rocket) or energy drops (D), name the shift you'll make for the second half. Write one sentence in your "Challenges" field about whether you adjusted or stayed the course.

The One-Thing Rule
When the plan isn't working — name the unlock
During ride
▾
When your horse isn't meeting your training plan — tight jaw, resistance, distraction — identify ONE thing that will unlock the rest. Not five things. For Rocket, Martin's lessons show it's almost always "soften the jaw through over-bending for a stride or two." For D, it's "inside seat bone forward, inside leg almost in his armpit." Name your one thing before you escalate effort. Post-ride, log whether it worked.

🔄
The Named Shift
Checkpoint with an action attached
During ride
▾
Week 1's checkpoint was "pause and ask." This week: the pause must produce a named decision. Not "I'll see how it goes" — a specific shift. "I'm reducing collection demands for the second half." "I'm switching to conditioning work because Rocket's jaw is still tight at 20 minutes." Name the adjustment before you make it, then log whether it was the right call.
📖 Building from Week 1
The mid-ride pause becomes a decision point. Your data shows you make good adjustments when you name them consciously — the challenge is doing it before quality degrades, not after.
